#### Teaching advice
Relative size of units in place value
Students should be provided with multiple opportunities to develop an understanding of place value relationships, such as 10 thousand  100 hundreds  1000 tens  10 000 ones. To be useful in estimation, place value relationships need to be well understood conceptually (Van de Walle et al. 2019).
For example, to estimate 894 × 3, we recognise the answer must be a little less than 900 × 3 but more than 800 × 3. Determining the product of 900 and 3, and 800 and 3 draws on place value units. The answer must be between 2400 and 2700, but closer to 2700. Similarly, 894 × 30 will be in a range 10 times greater (ie between 24 000 and 27 000).
See Stage 2 Teaching advice Representing numbers using place value A for further support in developing place value relationships.
Decimals
Decimals, like fractions, allow us to describe parts of a complete whole. When writing a fraction, the numerator and denominator enable us to show both the number of parts and the size of the parts. Decimals show only the number of parts, and we must determine the size of those parts using our knowledge of place value.
It is a reasonable expectation for students to learn how whole numbers work before understanding decimals. However, a complete appreciation of whole number place value is not a prerequisite to appreciating decimal place value. A student can show an understanding of the relative size of decimals and yet be unsure of the number 1 more than 3099.
Although decimals can be represented using a range of models to establish the relationship between the whole unit and its fractional components, linear models have been found to be particularly effective. It is easier to visually compare lengths than it is to compare area or volume.
Figure 1: A linear model of decimals
Image long description: 2 plastic rods of the same length form a line. Underneath 2 plastic rods of the same length and 3 small rods form a line. In front of this 0.203 is written on paper and 0.23 is written on another piece of paper.
Common units of measurement provide good models of decimals. Tape measures typically show a metre divided into tenths, hundredths (centimetres) and thousandths (millimetres).
Zero as a place holder
The role of zero as a place holder assists in understanding how we say and write decimals. Zero is written in the ones place in a decimal to reduce the risk of misreading the decimal as a whole number. The number 0.2 has the same value as 0.20 and the number 2 has the same value as 2.0.
In measurement, zeros at the end of a decimal have a different meaning as they are used to record precision. A measurement recorded as 0.2 seconds is measured in tenths of a second. A measurement recorded as 0.20 seconds is measured in hundredths of a second.
Decimal misconceptions
Some students view the decimal separator as a space between whole numbers. This misinterpretation may be observed when partitioning decimals.
For example, often students complete 5.37 = 5 + 0.3 + ___ by writing 7 rather than 0.07.
Another decimal misconception is associated with the role of zero. When zero is in the tenths place, students sometimes ignore it and treat the following digit as if it is in the tenths place (eg 0.07 is the same as 0.7). Some students assume that adding a zero on the end of a decimal increases its magnitude (eg, 0.320 is greater than 0.32) (Durkin and Rittle-Johnson 2015).
Further information on common decimal misconceptions can be found under Decimal misconceptions in Stage 2.

Language
In some Asian languages, such as Chinese, Japanese and Korean, the natural language structures used when expressing numbers larger than 10 000 are 'tens of thousands' rather than 'thousands', and 'tens of millions' rather than 'millions'.
For example, in Chinese (Mandarin), 612 000 is expressed as '61 wàn, 2 qiān', which translates as '61 tens of thousands and 2 thousands'. A change in thinking from 5-digit numbers in these languages to English number names (transcoding) may need practice and support.

Strategy choice
The strategies students use to carry out addition and subtraction develop from counting to using known number facts and properties. Some strategies are more efficient than others.
To solve 183 – 96 by making 183 marks and then crossing off 96 before counting the remaining marks, is an inefficient strategy. This method is sometimes described as ‘counting from one three times’. Although it ultimately arrives at the answer, this strategy takes too long and is prone to errors.
Whether a strategy is appropriate depends on the task or question itself. To be able to select appropriate strategies to add and subtract, students need to be aware that the numbers in a problem influence which methods are easier for that particular problem.
Sometimes it is easier to use addition for subtraction than to subtract the smaller number. To determine 2003 − 1997, finding the difference by adding 3 to 1997 to make 2000 and 3 more to 2003, establishes the answer of 6.
To foster choice and flexibility, students need opportunities to identify different strategies, and what makes them appropriate. Many methods involving addition and subtraction take advantage of one number’s proximity to a multiple of 10 or 100.

Order of operations
Without having an agreed order in which to carry out operations, expressions like 2 + 3 × 4 could result in two different answers, 20 or 14. However, if we agree that 3 × 4 means 4 + 4 + 4 then 2 + 3 × 4 is the same as 2 + 4 + 4 + 4, or 14. The multiplication is effectively ‘unfinished business’ and needs to be done first.
To reduce the risk of misunderstanding what is meant by an arithmetical expression, we have agreed on a convention. This convention is known as the order of operations. In brief, we complete all multiplications and divisions working left to right before adding and subtracting. If we want to change this order or to make certain the order is clear, we use grouping symbols to indicate what to do first.
An agreed order of operations means grouping symbols may not be needed to make the meaning clear, eg 32 + (2 ✕ 4) has the same answer as 32 + 2 ✕ 4.
The order of operations to perform calculations involving mixed operations and grouping symbols is as follows:
|  |  |
|---|---|
| 32 + 2 − 4 = 34 − 4= 30 | addition and subtraction only, work from left to right |
| 32 ÷ 2 ✕ 4 = 16 ✕ 4= 64 | multiplication and division only, work from left to right |
| 32 ÷ (2 ✕ 4) = 32 ÷ 8= 4 | perform operation inside the grouping symbols first |
| (32 + 2) ✕ 4 = 34 ✕ 4= 136 | perform operation in grouping symbols first |
| 32 + 2 ✕ 4 = 32 + 8= 40 | perform multiplication before addition |
Mnemonics like BOMDAS, BIDMAS or PEMDAS are often misleading as they suggest an absolute order between addition (A) and subtraction (S) or multiplication (M) and division (D).
A further limitation is that the second term in the mnemonic corresponding to powers and roots (Orders, Indices or Exponents) is taught in the Stage 4 curriculum.
Students can be supported in understanding the conventions by constructing anchor charts or using drawings or sentences to assist in recalling the agreed-upon order.

Language
The terms used with fractions reflect both the richness of their history and the challenges of printing fractions. Initially, the association between fractions and division was very close. #### Content

#### Teaching advice
3D objects
Prisms and pyramids
In Stage 3, the formal names for particular prisms and pyramids are introduced while students are engaged in their construction and representation. Only 'family' names, such as prism, were introduced in Stage 2.
Students in Stage 3 learn to name prisms and pyramids according to the shape of their base. They should be familiar with at least the square pyramid and the triangular pyramid.
Another name for a triangular pyramid is a tetrahedron. If a triangular pyramid has all its triangular faces identical, it is called a regular tetrahedron.
Figure 1: A net of a regular tetrahedron
Prisms have two bases that are the same shape and size. The bases of a prism may be squares, rectangles, triangles or other polygons. The other faces are rectangular if the faces are perpendicular to the bases. The base of a prism is the shape of the uniform cross-section, not necessarily the face on which it is resting.
In geometry, a three-dimensional object is called a solid. The three-dimensional object may in fact be hollow, but it is still defined as a geometrical solid.
Visualising, spatial imagery and 2D representations
Visualising refers to creating a mental image. A mental image can be formed from a range of sensory inputs (eg visual, auditory, tactile). Students should have opportunities to explore the features of 3D objects with their hands, without looking at the object (eg in a bag or behind a screen) to help build more robust mental images that focus on the structure of the object rather than its appearance.
In understanding the role played by spatial imagery when solving mathematics problems, it is important to recognise it is not limited to the visual modality. It is possible to have a spatial image formed by touch or even by reflected sound.
Lack of access to the visual field should not diminish a person’s ability to visualise. Spatial imagination amongst those who do not see with their eyes relies on tactile and auditory activity (Healy and Fernandes 2014).
There are two common ways of representing spatial information: schematically or pictorially.
Schematic representations encode the spatial relations described in a problem. A schematic representation is like the plan of a house.
Pictorial representations encode the visual appearance of the objects described in the problem.
Figure 2: A pictorial representation of the problem
Figure 3: A schematic representation of the problem
Use of schematic spatial representations has been associated with success in mathematical problem solving, whereas use of pictorial representations was negatively correlated with success (Hegarty and Kozhevnikov 1999).
Schematic representations tend to reduce the amount of information and emphasise mathematical relationships more than pictorial forms.
Representing the spatial relationships between objects and imagining spatial transformations appears to be more helpful in mathematics than holding or representing vivid and detailed pictorial images (Hegarty and Waller 2005).
Spatial reasoning is important in solving problems using 2D representations of 3D prisms (Fujita et al. 2020).
Volume and capacity as a form of volume
Capacity refers to the amount a container can hold (internal volume) and is measured in units, such as millilitres (mL), litres (L) and kilolitres (kL). Capacity is only used in relation to containers and generally refers to liquid measurement (although internal volume can be measured with cubic units as well). The capacity of a closed container will be slightly less than its volume – capacity is based on the inside dimensions, while volume is determined by the outside dimensions of the container. It is not necessary to refer to these definitions with students in K-6 (capacity is not taught as a concept separate from volume until Stage 4).

Although the abbreviation ‘cc’ is sometimes used for cubic centimetres, it is not internationally accepted. The internationally accepted abbreviation is cm3.

### Teaching advice for Non-spatial measure A
#### Content

#### Teaching advice
Mass
Mass is an intrinsic property of an object, but its most common measure is in terms of weight. Weight is a force that changes with gravity, while mass remains constant. The weight of a body is usually of more practical interest than its mass.
In day-to-day use, the word weight is a synonym for mass. In scientific use, weight means mechanical force, the product of mass and acceleration due to gravity.
The kilogram was originally defined by a single block of platinum-iridium alloy, known as the International Prototype Kilogram (IPK). The IPK and its duplicates were used to calibrate all other mass standards on the Earth. In 2019 the formal definition of a kilogram changed to be based on a physical constant value.
All goods sold by weight in Australia use the metric units associated with mass, grams and kilograms. Consequently, the focus in Mathematics K–6 is on the metric units of measure for mass, rather than the interaction between mass and gravitational force.
Gross mass is the mass of both the contents of a container and the container. Net mass is the mass of the contents only.
Local industries and businesses could provide sources for the study of measurement in tonnes, eg weighbridges, cranes, hoists.
Time
Although 24-hour time can be recorded without the use of the colon (:), it is frequently used to separate hours and minutes, eg 3:45 pm is written as 15:45.
Some Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander students may require support in code switching between knowledges of time from a cultural perspective and Eastern Standard Australian Time concepts.
Language
The terms 'weigh' and 'weight' are common in everyday usage. Even the Australian National Measurement Act uses the term ‘weights and measures’ rather than mass. Although the term ‘mass’ is used throughout the syllabus, students should learn that in day-to-day usage the mass of an object is referred to as its weight.

### Teaching advice for Data A
#### Content
Data A
#### Teaching advice
Types of data
The characteristics or attributes that you observe, measure and record such as eye colour or height are called variables.
Data collected for a variable that can be separated into distinct groups, eg colour or blood type is called categorical. Column graphs are useful in recording categorical data, including results obtained from probability experiments.
Ordinal data is a kind of categorical data which exists in ordered or ranked categories. Like positions in a race, first, second and third, the order is clear but not the distance between each position.
Numerical data values are expressed as numbers and obtained by counting, or by measurement of a physical attribute, eg the number of students in a class (count) or the heights of students in a class (measurement).
Counts are discrete values and measurements of physical attributes are continuous values. Column graphs can be used for discrete data values but should not be used to represent continuous data values.
Scale
A scale of many-to-one correspondence in a column graph or line graph means that one unit is used to represent more than one of what is being counted or measured, eg 1 cm on the vertical axis could be used to represent 20 cm of body height.
Line graphs
Line graphs are best used where meaning can be attached to the points on the line between plotted points, eg temperature readings over time.
Figure 1: A line graph showing growth over time
Image long description: A line chart showing growth over time. The left axis shows height in cm from 0 to 16. The bottom axis shows days from 1 to 10. The plotted line goes from 0 cm on days 1 and 2 and increases over the days to reach 15 cm on day 10.
Line graphs should not be used with discrete data. Discrete data is best represented using a column graph.
Figure 2: A column graph used with categorical data
Image long description: A column graph showing colours in a pack. The left vertical axis lists numbers from 0 to 9. The bottom horizontal axis lists colours red at 8, orange at 6, yellow at 7, green at 6, blue at 7, mauve at 7, pink at 6 and brown at 5.
Different representations of the same data can result in different features of the data being highlighted or obscured.
For example, if you wish to see change in weight over time, a line graph is more effective than a picture graph showing units of weight*.
Although some software programs display data in a 3D representation, or ‘3D charts’, these displays are misleading due to the slanting perspective. 3D charts are best avoided when presenting data.
* Picture graphs are sometimes described as pictograms or pictographs.
Language
The word ‘data’ is technically the plural form of the Latin term, and ‘datum’ is used to refer to a specific piece of information. However, the use of ‘datum’ has declined over time and today ‘data’ is commonly used as a singular mass noun.

### Teaching advice for Data B
#### Content
Data B
#### Teaching advice
Interpreting data
In Stage 3 students are provided with opportunities to describe the characteristics of graphs. In particular, the data can be described in terms of its distribution: how far the data is spread out (range), where most of the data points are (mode) and how the data is arranged visually. Students can also discuss whether the data are clustered. In Stage 4 students learn about ways of describing the centre of the data (median and arithmetic mean).
Data selected for interpretation might include census data, environmental audits of resources such as water and energy, and sports statistics.
Language
In day-to-day use bias refers to a prejudice in favour of or against something, usually in a way considered to be unfair. In statistics, bias is essentially when something is unrepresentative of the population.
There are different sources of bias that can arise in the collection and analysis of data. One common form of bias is selection bias. If you only ask the opinions of your friends, the opinions will not be representative of the wider population.
In Stage 3 students need to recognise that data can present a biased view if it is not representative or is poorly displayed.

### Teaching advice for Chance A
#### Content
Chance A
#### Teaching advice
The numeric value assigned to the probability of chance events occurring can be ordered on a scale from 0 to 1. A probability of 0 describes the probability of an event that is impossible. A probability of 1 describes the probability of an event that must occur.
A numeric value is an exact statement of probability whereas the language associated with probability remains imprecise. A weather report might indicate a 100% chance of no rain as being ‘very high’ rather than certain.
Coin tossing is common in many sports and has on occasion been used to settle disputes. For a fair coin, landing on heads or tails is equally likely. This means heads coming up on the toss of a fair coin can be described as having a probability of  (or 0.5 or 50%).
Students’ experience when ordering fractions and decimals on a number line from 0 to 1 can support their work on comparing measures of probability.
The sum of the probabilities of the outcomes of a chance experiment where one of the outcomes must occur, is equal to 1.
Language
The probability of an outcome is the value (between 0 and 1) used to describe the chance that the outcome will occur.
The term ‘simple’ when used in conjunction with probability or an event, describes the probability of a single event occurring. Obtaining a head on a single toss of coin is a simple event whereas obtaining two heads on two tosses is not. Students do further work on solving problems involving the probabilities of simple chance experiments in Stage 4.
A list of all the outcomes for a chance experiment is known as the 'sample space'; however, this term is not introduced until Stage 4.
The singular form of dice was originally die. However, modern English now considers dice as both singular and plural nouns.

### Teaching advice for Chance B
#### Content
Chance B
#### Teaching advice
Randomness
Events vary in their degree of predictability or independence. Some events are unpredictable or random. If these events occur together, it does not mean that they are necessarily related or cause each other.
Events occurring together can sometimes lead to the belief that they must be related – a fallacy described as ‘with this therefore because of this’.
Random generators include coins, dice and spinners. When a random generator, like rolling dice, is used to produce data, probability describes the variation we expect to see in repeated samples from the same population.
Reasoning about probability
Probabilistic reasoning is the connection of proportional thinking with the idea of randomness. Understanding the differing proportions of possible outcomes sets up the expectations (theoretical probability). Understanding randomness explains the variation of actual outcomes (experimental probability).
As the number of trials in a chance experiment increases, the observed probabilities tend to become closer in value to the expected probabilities.
Monte Carlo methods
Monte Carlo methods or Monte Carlo experiments are the names given to using repeated random sampling to approximate the theoretical probability. These chance experiments are useful in situations where we are uncertain about or cannot calculate the theoretical probabilities.
For example, using collected data to approximate the likelihood of each landing position when tossing a paper cup.
Language
The term 'frequency' is used in Chance and Data to describe the number of times a particular outcome occurs in a chance experiment.
The word ‘random’ can have several meanings outside of mathematics. It can mean something unknown, lacking uniformity, unpredictable, or out of place.
