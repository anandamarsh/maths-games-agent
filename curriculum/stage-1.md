### Teaching advice for Representing whole numbers A
#### Content
Representing whole numbers A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Teachers should encourage students to develop efficient grouping strategies when counting large collections. For example, they can count out groups of five or ten and then find the total using the groups. Grouping makes it easier to check the total.
By combining quantities in a variety of ways, students can learn there are more efficient ways to total large collections than counting one by one.
Teaching advice
A unit of ten things is considered to be a composite unit if it can be interpreted as both one 10 (a single unit) and ten individual units at the same time.
Numeral zero
The numeral 0 plays two separate roles in reading numbers:
a lexical tag on the end of a number generating its name, eg 20, 100
internal zeros used as place holders, eg 107.
The number line
The number line can be a powerful model for thinking about quantities. To be most effective, the number line needs to be introduced when students can make use of its defining feature – length.
When numeral cards are placed in order side by side they form a numeral track. A numeral track is an ordered sequence of whole numbers. It acts as a counting model because the numerals in the track support counting.
The number line is a more sophisticated model of number. It introduces the idea of distance between numbers. The number line is a model of length. With a number line, students have to count the length units and not the numerals.
Figure 4: An example of a number line (0–9) with a length of 5 shown from zero to 5.
In Geometric measure, students are encouraged to make a measuring device with uniform units before using a ruler. A similar approach can be used to introduce the number line.
Students need both knowledge of numerals and the quantities they represent to use number lines.
Language
In Australia, the word and is used when reading a number greater than 100 or writing it in words.
For example, five hundred and sixty-three.
Students should be made aware that when numbers are used as labels they are said differently. For example:
bus numbers
postcodes
telephone numbers.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
count forwards/count backwards
number before/number after
more than/less than
number line/numeral track
digit
zero
tens/groups of ten
round to
counters
coins
dollars/cents
odd/even.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
Acquiring an understanding of whole numbers does not develop uniformly. As well as learning the sequence of number names for multiples of 10 to successfully cross the decades, students need experience with the sequence beyond 100.
It is not unusual for students to be uncertain of what number follows 109 (Gould 2017). Teachers may use the following strategies to determine if a student can identify the repeating pattern of counting numbers beyond 100.
Ask the student: What number comes after 109?
Alternatively, ask the student to: Start counting from 106 and stop at 120.
As students need to learn how to represent numbers using numerals, you could also ask them to record the number that comes after 109. Answers like ‘10010’ for one hundred and ten are attempts at literal transcoding.

### Teaching advice for Representing whole numbers B
#### Content
Representing whole numbers B
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Place value is an important and fundamental concept in early number work. An appreciation of the structure of tens and hundreds forms the foundation for operating with two-digit and three-digit numbers. The focus of representing whole numbers in Stage 1 is developing a robust understanding of the composite units of 10 and 100 that make up two-digit and three-digit numbers.
Teaching advice
Place value
Rather than describing a single property (such as positional value) our place value system has a number of different properties (Ross 2002) or aspects. This includes the following properties:
Additive property: The quantity represented by the whole numeral is the sum of the values represented by the individual digits.
Positional property: The quantities represented by the individual digits are determined by the position that they hold in the whole numeral.
Base-ten property: The values of the positions increase in powers of ten from right to left.
Multiplicative property: The value of an individual digit is found by multiplying the face value of the digit by the value assigned to its position.
An understanding of place value numeration develops over time and may include an understanding of:
tens and ones
regrouping and renaming
decimal representation.
Place value understanding needs to be explicitly developed in a range of contexts involving operating with numbers.
Working with two-digit and three-digit numbers
A unit of ten things is considered to be a composite unit if it can be interpreted as both one 10 (a single unit) and ten individual units at the same time.
The focus of representing whole numbers in Stage 1 is developing a robust understanding of the composite units of 10 and 100 that make up two-digit and three-digit numbers.
Using number charts
There are many ways that number charts can be used to support student understanding of the base-ten numeration system (Bay-Williams and Fletcher 2017). Number charts either start at 0 or 1 and can provide opportunities for students to notice:
ten as 10 ones and 1 ten
one hundred as 10 tens
patterns, such as 10 more.
Modern number charts often do not stop at 100 but continue to 120. This provides opportunities for students to recognise how the counting pattern continues beyond 100. Some charts also start at the bottom and build up, to model how the process of adding makes higher totals (going up the chart).
Identifying the nearest multiple of ten
Many procedures used when rounding numbers emphasise the digit values more than the units of ten and hundred, eg rounding up if the last digit is five or more.
Instead of teaching procedures for rounding to the nearest 10 or 100, place the emphasis on:
developing the quantity value of numbers
identifying the nearest ten (or hundred) to a number.
Using Number talks to assess student reasoning
Number talks (Parrish 2011) are brief discussions that focus on how students solve a problem in their heads. They encourage students to communicate their reasoning. Number talks may be useful in assessing students’ understanding to reason about representations of whole numbers to 1000.
When using number talks, it is important to ensure all students can participate. Incremental strategies such as think-pair-share can be effective. Students learning English as an additional language or dialect, including Aboriginal students, can be supported by using home-language buddies to engage in the number talks. Students from a range of cultural backgrounds may be more comfortable sharing their thinking as part of a pair or small group, rather than individually. Some students with disability communicate through a variety of verbal or nonverbal communication systems or techniques. The problems used in a number talk should have more than one method of finding the answer.
Number talks that use the principles of a yarning circle or mathematical journals can provide opportunities to engage with mathematical storytelling. Students share their solutions, while the teacher models recording their thinking. Students should be provided with opportunities to engage with mathematics using storytelling, such as recounting a method or strategy used to solve a problem.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to investigate if a student’s understanding of the positional value of the digits aligns with the cardinal value of the numeral.
Digit-correspondence task: 16 tiles
The following is an example of a digit-correspondence task – a style of question frequently used by Ross (2002), a researcher in children’s numerical thinking.
Ask the student to count a number of items, typically between 10 and 40, and write or identify the numeral corresponding to the total. The student is then asked to show with the items how the digits in the recorded number represent the total.
S: [Writes 16.]
T: So that number is?
S: Sixteen.
T: And there are how many here? [Points to the 16 tiles.]
S: Sixteen.
T: OK. So with the number 16 … this 6 here [points to the 6 in the 16] can you show me with the tiles how you would make that?
S: [Makes 6 tiles as 3 and 3.] Three and three.
T: Let’s put them on the 6 side.
S: [Moves the tiles to the right of the digit 6.]
T: Great. And this 1 here [points to the 1 in 16] … what would that mean for the tiles?
S: For the units [moves one tile next to the 1] … it stands for the units in the tens and ones.
T: Tens and ones. Ok. Is this 1 here [points to the 1 in 16] does that mean a unit or something else?
S: A unit.
T: [Revoices.] A unit. So altogether [points to the 7 tiles on the paper near the written 16 and circles them in the air] there are how many here?
S: Seven.
T: [Revoices.] Seven. Now if there are 7 here and this number is 16 and there are 16 tiles altogether, what happens to these tiles? [Points to the remaining 9 tiles.]
S: They are the leftovers.
This student’s understanding of the positional value of the digits does not align with the cardinal value of the numeral.

### Teaching advice for Combining and separating quantities A
#### Content
Combining and separating quantities A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Addition and subtraction typically progress from counting, combining and separating perceptual objects to using numbers as replacements for completed counts.
Teaching advice
Students in Stage 1 can use concrete materials and drawings:
to model and solve problems
for exploration
for concept building.
The recording students make in combining and separating quantities can also provide support for their mental strategies by reducing the load on working memory.
Advanced count-by-ones methods
The first advanced count-by-ones strategies students use for addition and subtraction are counting on and counting back. That is, students:
count on from the larger number to find the total of two numbers
count back from a number to find the quantity remaining
count on or back to find the difference between two numbers.
As outlined briefly in the teaching advice for Early Stage 1, subtraction covers two different situations. Subtraction can involve:
taking away from a group, or
comparison (determining how many more or fewer when comparing two groups).
Counting on or back
In performing a comparison subtraction, students could use counting on or back from one number to find the difference. This type of subtraction can be more difficult for students to grasp than the taking away type. However, counting on or back can be an efficient method of solving comparison problems when using larger numbers.
Once students are confident with the taking away type, encourage them to use counting on or back where the differences are small (less than five). For example, when comparing 19 with 17, it is easier to count on from 17 than take away 17 objects from a collection of 19.
Using number bonds
Number bonds are pairs of numbers that bond through addition to form another number. Number pairs that bond to form 10 are particularly useful when adding or subtracting. However, students may know number bonds but not use them to add and subtract.
Flexible strategies
Using flexible strategies refers to methods that do not rely on counting by one. Flexible strategies include using known number combinations and bridging to 10, as well as methods of adjusting the numbers in the question without changing the result (sometimes called compensation).
Students who can recall number bonds may still rely on strategies like counting on to solve problems. This is why it is important to model the use of number bonds in addition and subtraction problems.
Bridging to 10
Teachers can begin to model the use of number bonds in bridging to 10 once students are familiar with the number bonds.
For example, 28 + 7 is the same as 28 + 2 + 5 (partitioning the 7 into 2 plus 5 to bridge to the next multiple of ten). This uses the number bond 8 + 2 = 10 as well as partitioning 7.
It is important to explicitly model the use of number bonds in bridging to 10. Many students do not use the number bonds they know to add and subtract (Graven and Venkat 2021).
Quantity value
As well as number bonds, students’ informal mental calculation strategies with two-digit numbers substantially rely on quantity values. Quantity value involves using the value of the parts in a standard partition of a number greater than 10 separately (eg 43 as 40 and 3).
Mental calculation strategies treat the digits in the tens or hundreds column as a quantity in their own right. The 4 in 345 is forty rather than a four in the tens column (or even four tens). Place value has many aspects. The former is the quantity value aspect of place value and the latter the column value aspect.
Language
Difference
In everyday language, the word difference can refer to any attribute. However, it has a specific meaning in number questions, where it means the distance between two numbers on a number line.
Students should develop an understanding that the process of subtraction can be indicated by a variety of language structures. The language used in the comparison type of subtraction can be different from that used in the take away type. For example, how might a teacher say ‘9 minus 3’?
| Comparison language | Take away language |
|---|---|
| What is 3 subtracted from 9? | Reduce 9 by 3. |
| By how much does 9 exceed 3? | From 9 take 3. |
| What number is 3 less than 9? | Take 3 from 9. |
| What is the difference between 9 and 3? | 9 take away 3. |
| How many added to 3 make 9? | Decrease 9 by 3. |
| How many more than 3 is 9? | From 9 subtract 3. |
The many different ways of expressing ‘9 minus 3’ highlights the futility of trying to teach a key word approach to interpreting mathematics questions.
Left
The word ‘left’ can be confusing for students. For example:
There were five children in the room. Three went to lunch. How many were left?
Words with the same spelling but different meanings (homographs) are particularly challenging for students learning English as an additional language or dialect.
Word order
It is not simply the vocabulary used in mathematics that can make questions challenging. Word order is crucial. Consider the following two questions:
Four is half of what number? (8)
What number is half of four? (2)
These two questions use exactly the same words but ask for entirely different responses.

counting on/counting back
combine/plus/add
take away/minus
the difference between
total
increase/decrease
more than/less than
bridge

double

strategy.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to investigate if a student can use the cardinal value of numbers in calculating.
A teacher might present the following scenario:
I have 13 cards and Kim gave me some more. Altogether, I now have 16 cards.
The teacher could then ask:
How many cards did Kim give me?
A student is using the cardinal value of a number like 13 when there is no need to count from 1 to form 13. Recognising that 16 is 3 more (counts) makes use of the additive property. Each counting number represents a part–whole relation.

### Teaching advice for Combining and separating quantities B
#### Content
Combining and separating quantities B
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
In Stage 1, students develop a range of strategies using recall of number bonds to make 10 and solve addition and subtraction problems. An understanding of the meaning of the equality sign (=) is considered the basis of relational thinking in algebra. When thinking relationally about the equality symbol, students consider the number sentence as a whole, before analysing the component parts and the operations involved. This interpretation sets students up for success in algebra.
Teaching advice
Understanding place value
Calculating with multi-digit numbers does not need to wait until students have fully mastered place value. It typically takes years before students are able to integrate the different aspects of place value. Research confirms (National Research Council 2001) that students can develop an understanding of both the base-ten system and procedures for calculating when they have opportunities to explore how and why the procedures work.
Encourage students to explain their strategies in solving addition and subtraction problems and to develop ways of recording their actions. Discuss the merits of various strategies in terms of how practical and efficient they are.
Recording mental strategies using an empty number line
Using structured quantitative models like bead strings or linking cubes can help students see how addition and subtraction of numbers can be recorded. When using bead strings, it is important that students are familiar with the way that colour is used on the bead string to show groups of 5. When students are familiar with the structure of these models, combining and separating quantities can be linked to recording on an empty number line. Below, the bead string is repeated as 5 red bead strings, followed by 5 white bead strings.
Activity: Forming 8 and 5.
Activity: Combining 8 and 5, aligned to an empty number line.
Jump strategy on a number line
In this addition or subtraction strategy the student places the first number on an empty number line. They then move forwards or backwards to perform the calculation, often first by tens and then by ones. Encourage students to make use of multiples of 10 and number bonds. These require fewer jumps and are more efficient.
An example of using the jump strategy method for addition: 46 + 33
An example of using the jump strategy method for subtraction: 79 – 33
Separate 10s and 1s
In this strategy, sometimes called a split strategy, the student separates the tens from the ones (units). Each is added separately and then combined to find the final answer.
A common error when this method is used for subtraction questions is the smaller from larger error. For example, separating the tens in 96 – 58 results in 90 – 50. Some students may then swap the order for the units 8 – 6. This produces an incorrect answer of 42. For this reason, the split strategy should only be used for addition.
Inverse strategy
An inverse operation reverses the effect of the original operation. The shopkeeper’s method of giving change uses the inverse strategy of addition to perform a subtraction. Because addition and subtraction are inverse operations a student can add up from the smaller number to the larger number to obtain the answer. 
For example, using the inverse strategy to solve 65 – 37 would proceed as follows:
Start at 37
Add 3 to make 40
Then add 20 to make 60
Finally add 5 to make 65
And so the answer is 3 + 20 + 5 = 28
Equality
Students need to understand the different uses for the equality sign (=). Many students interpret = as a signal to perform an operation.
For example, when read aloud, 4 + 1 = 5 is often stated as four plus one equals five. While this is consistent with left-to-right decoding when reading text, it does not promote an understanding of the relational nature of the equality sign.
Students need to:
interpret the equal sign relationally
recognise that the two sides can be exchanged.
To assist students in understanding the equality sign (=), teachers can:
vary how number sentences are recorded
read the relational symbol as is the same amount as.
The number sentence 5 = 4 + 1 is also a valid statement of equality. Five is the same amount as four plus one.
Gesturing
Gesturing when teaching can help students appreciate the two sides in an equality relationship. For example:
I want to make one side [sweeping left hand back and forth under the left side of the problem] equal to the other side [sweeping right hand back and forth under the right side of the problem].
This can encourage students to produce similar gestures, which can aid learning.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following questions can be used to determine if a student can reason about the relationship between addition and subtraction to solve problems involving partitioning.
Bridging to the next multiple of 10
By the end of Stage 1, students should typically be able to use their knowledge of number bonds of ten to solve addition and subtraction problems such as:
I have $23. How much more do I need to have $30?
I have $34. How much more do I need to have $40?
Knowledge of number bonds (for example combinations of ten) as known facts is only useful when students can apply this knowledge to solve other tasks.
Bridging through the next multiple of 10
By the end of Stage 1, students should typically be able to bridge to 10 to solve two-digit addition and subtraction problems, such as:
I have $44. How much more do I need to have $51? How did you work it out?
I have $25. How much more do I need to have $32? How did you work it out?
Students should use number bonds up to ten to solve addition and subtraction problems. Number bonds forming seven (eg 6 and 1) assist in solving 44 + _ = 51. Similarly, number bonds forming seven (eg 5 and 2) can assist in solving 25 + _ = 32.
Bridging to the next multiple of ten and bridging through the next multiple of ten are foundational combining and partitioning strategies. Revisit these strategies regularly for consolidation.
Students should develop these flexible strategies when dealing with one-digit and two-digit number problems before exposure to two-digit and two-digit addition/subtraction problems (where the difference is greater than 20).
While the context of money has been used, teachers can adapt these questions and change the context. The difference between the values in the following questions is more than five to discourage counting on.
Reasoning about quantity linking addition and subtraction
The following questions are designed to investigate students’ reasoning about the relation between addition and subtraction. This type of reasoning underpins compensation and estimation methods. It continues to develop in Stage 2. The questions are intended for use after students have had an opportunity to learn the inverse relationship between addition and subtraction.
Strategies and questions
Addition to related subtraction: Can you use this: 27 + 13 = 40 [display the number sentence] to work out 40 – 13 [display the question]?

### Teaching advice for Forming groups A
#### Content
Forming groups A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Forming groups helps students progress from counting by ones to skip counting and using repeated addition. These are important steps towards the idea of coordinating units and understanding multiplication.
Teaching advice
Rhythmic and skip counting
Number words sequences for fives and tens produce a rhythm. This rhythm can be used in choral counting. Skip counting by two can develop from counting aloud and:
stressing each second number: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 …
skipping over the non-stressed numbers: 2, 4, 6 …
Division
There are two forms of division associated with forming groups:
| Division | Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Equal Sharing | Partitive | How many in each group? | If 12 marbles are shared between three students, how many does each student get? |
| Equal grouping | Quotitive | How many groups are there? | If I have 12 marbles and each child is to get four, how many children will get marbles? |
Understanding grouping division is important to make sense of dividing by a quantity less than one (eg 8 ÷ 0·5) which students encounter in Stage 4.
After students have divided a quantity into equal groups (eg twelve objects divided into groups of four) they should recombine the groups. This reversal of the process links multiplication and division.
When sharing a collection of objects into two groups, students may describe the number of objects in each group as being one half of the whole collection.
Arrays
An array is one of several different arrangements that can be used to model multiplicative situations involving whole numbers.
An array is made by arranging a set of objects, such as counters or pictures, into columns and rows:
each column must contain the same number of items as the other columns
each row must contain the same number of items as the other rows.
Students should focus on using groups of, before progressing to rows of and columns of. Although the array is a powerful mathematical model, it requires students to identify groups in two directions. Coordinating between columns and rows is much more difficult than using groups of with repeated addition.
Composite units
A composite unit is a unit-whole composed of other units. A unit of 10 things is a composite unit if it can be interpreted as both one 10 (a single unit) and 10 individual units at the same time. A model of a composite unit would be one pea pod containing 10 peas.
In developing an understanding of a group acting as composite it is helpful to distinguish between the number of groups and the number in each group as this can be confusing for students.
Introducing fractions: Equal sharing (linked with division)
Many students find understanding fractions, and in particular fraction magnitudes, one of the most challenging concepts in learning mathematics.
Fractions are introduced through equal-sharing problems. As Empson and Levi (2011) stated, ‘If models do not draw on children’s formative experiences of sharing and partitioning, then they are likely to prevent teachers from cultivating the natural insights about quantities that young children have’.
Students can later connect the words and symbols for fractions to the parts created in the process of solving the problems. The concept of a fraction is then based on the process of division, rather than counting parts. This lays the foundations for fractions as indicating division and fractions as numbers.
Equal sharing begins with sharing between two. The process of halving is applied in early models of halving lengths (see Geometric measure) and discrete quantities in forming groups. To assist students in seeing the link between the two models, linear arrangements of discrete quantities are used.
Some students may have prior knowledge and understanding of fractions and dividing objects equally among groups. For example, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students may have an understanding of the concept of fractions as it relates to kinship and community responsibilities.
Language
Check that students are familiar with groups of two like things being called pairs.
Avoid using the term ‘lots of’ when describing grouping for multiplication. It can be confusing to students because of its everyday use, for example ‘lots of fish in the sea'.
Formal writing of number sentences for multiplication and division, including the use of the symbols × and ÷ is not introduced until Stage 2.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to determine if a student can use the structure of equal groups to solve multiplication problems and group for division (this activity was also described in Early Stage 1 Forming groups).
[Provide students with a pile of at least 20 unifix cubes]
Can you make 5 towers, with 3 blocks in each tower?
How many towers do you have?
How many blocks in each tower?
How many altogether?

Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
group
number of groups/number in each group
row/column
number of rows/number of columns
number in each row/number in each column
array
sharing/shared between
left over
total
equal
half/quarter/eighth
one-half/one-quarter/one-eighth
halve.

### Teaching advice for Geometric measure A
#### Content
Geometric measure A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Being able to describe the relative positions of objects in a picture or diagram requires interpretation of a two-dimensional representation. In Stage 1, measuring the lengths of objects using uniform informal units enables students to develop some key understandings of measurement.
Teaching advice
Position
In Early Stage 1, students used the terms ‘left’ and ‘right’ to describe position in relation to themselves. In Stage 1, many students begin to use the terms ‘left’ and ‘right’ to describe position from the perspective of a person facing in the opposite direction.
Length
Understandings involved in measuring the lengths of objects include:
units should be repeatedly placed end to end without gaps or overlaps
units must be equal in size
identical units should be used to compare lengths
some units are more appropriate for measuring particular objects
there is a relationship between the size of the chosen unit and the number of units needed.
Make, mark and move
Using the phrase make, mark and move assists students in understanding the concept of repeated units. By placing a unit on a flat surface, marking where it ends, moving it along and continuing the process, students identify that the unit of measurement is the space between the marks on a measuring device and not the marks themselves.
Conserving length
Recognising that a length may be divided and recombined to form the same length is an important component of conserving length.
Estimating length
Estimating length is often based on visualising a known unit or benchmark. The units might be personal measures such as finger-widths or handspans.
Before students are asked to estimate lengths and distances it is important that students:
have had some measurement experiences
are taught a variety of estimation strategies.
To estimate longer distances, teachers may ask students to imagine how many times a metre ruler could be laid end to end across the space. To assist with this method of estimating, have one student measure out the distance being estimated with a metre ruler. Allow students to adjust their estimates after the first metre is measured and again at about halfway.
Students may have prior knowledge or experience with measurement and estimating length. For example, some Aboriginal students may have a sense of measurement based on their cultural knowledge or on-Country experiences. Teachers are encouraged to further develop their knowledge, and engage in meaningful exchange with students to support making connections with Western concepts of measurement.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
length
distance
end/end to end
side by side
gap
overlap
measure
estimate
unit
handspan.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to determine if a student can identify the halfway point of a journey. This question relates to both length and fractions and builds on the content in Early Stage 1.
An ant crawls around the outside of this triangle [teacher gestures around the sides of the triangle]:
If each side of the triangle is the same length, can you show where the ant will be when it is half of the way around the triangle from the start?
Starting from the base emphasises the need to find half of the distance around the triangle rather than halving the triangle.

### Teaching advice for Geometric measure B
#### Content
Geometric measure B
#### Teaching advice
Length
Formal units of length
Students should be given opportunities to apply their understanding of measurement, gained through experiences with the use of uniform informal units, to experiences with the use of the centimetre and metre.
They could make a measuring device using uniform informal units before using a ruler, eg using a length of 10 connecting cubes. This would assist students in understanding that the distances between marks on a ruler represent unit lengths and that the marks indicate the endpoints of each unit. Half of a length of 10 connecting cubes provides a link to simple fractions.
Fractional lengths
In Stage 1, fractions are formed by the process of division rather than counting parts. Fractional parts, halves, quarters and eighths are formed in two different ways:
equal parts of a whole length
equal quantities of a linear arrangement of a collection of objects.
In focusing on repeated halving, emphasise that the size of the resulting fractional part will depend on the size of the original whole length or collection of objects. It is not necessary for students in Stage 1 to use the symbol ½ to mean one-half or use ¼ for one-quarter.
Three models of fractions
Continuous model (linear)
The continuous model (linear) should be introduced first. It uses one-directional cuts or folds that compare fractional parts based on length. It is helpful to highlight the long edge to focus on length.
Continuous model (area)
This model uses multi-directional cuts or folds to compare fractional parts to the whole. This model should be introduced once students understand the concept of area developed in Stage 2. Folding to show equivalent area relies on a shape having an axis of symmetry for the fold line.
Discrete model
This model uses separate items in collections to represent parts of the whole group.
Language
When recording measurements, a space should be left between the number and the abbreviated unit, eg 3 cm, not 3cm.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
metre
centimetre.

### Teaching advice for Two-dimensional spatial structure A
#### Content
Two-dimensional spatial structure A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Manipulation of a variety of real objects and shapes is crucial to the development of appropriate levels of visualisation, language and representation. Also, measuring the areas of objects using informal units enables students to develop some key understandings of measurement. These include:
repeatedly placing units so that there are no gaps or overlaps
understanding that the units must be equal in size.
Teaching advice
2D shape
Representing and visualising three-dimensional objects and two-dimensional shapes are developing in Stage 1 and should be fostered through practical activities and communication.
Students should be able to recognise shapes presented in different orientations.
Students should have experiences identifying both regular shapes (all sides and angles equal) and irregular shapes. It is not expected that students understand or distinguish between regular and irregular shapes in Stage 1.
Area
Area relates to the measurement of two-dimensional space, in the same way that volume relates to the measurement of three-dimensional space. The attribute of area is the amount of surface (either flat or curved) and can be measured in square units:
square centimetres (cm2)
square metres (m2).
Informal units
Measuring the areas of objects using informal units enables students to develop some key understandings of measurement. These include:
repeatedly placing units so that there are no gaps or overlaps
understanding that the units must be equal in size.
Covering surfaces with a range of informal units should assist students in understanding that some units tessellate (fit together without gaps or overlaps) and are therefore more suitable for measuring area.
When students draw the covering of a rectangle with unit squares, some will draw each individual square while others will draw a combination of individual squares and lines. Some students will draw lines to represent rows and then mark off squares individually while others will draw an array using lines.
Grid structure
Students should be encouraged to make, draw and describe the spatial structure (grid) for rectangular shapes. To assist students in noticing the grid structure, outline cardboard square tiles.
Students should develop procedures for counting tile or grid units so that no units are missed or counted twice. They should be encouraged to identify and use efficient strategies for counting, eg using repeated addition or skip counting.
It is important that:
students have had some measurement experiences including cutting up and rearranging areas before being asked to estimate areas
a variety of estimation strategies is taught.
Language
2D shape
The term ‘vertex’ (plural: ‘vertices’) refers to the point where:
two straight sides of a two-dimensional shape meet
three or more faces of a three-dimensional object meet.
The term ‘shape’ refers to a two-dimensional figure. The term ‘object’ refers to a three-dimensional figure.
Students learn to identify a number of different polygons including:
triangles
quadrilaterals
pentagons
hexagons
octagons.
The names of these shapes reflect Latin and Greek influences:
Triangle: from the Latin triangulus, meaning three angles or corners. It is helpful to check if students know any other words where ‘tri’ also means three, such as tricycle or triceratops.
Quadrilateral: from the Latin quadrilaterus, meaning four sides or flanks. While squares and rectangles are examples of quadrilaterals, a square is also a special type of rectangle.
Polygon: comes from ancient Greek (written in the Latin alphabet) and means many angles. Pentagons (five angles), hexagons (six angles) and octagons (eight angles) are all examples of polygons.
A shape has line symmetry if matching parts are produced when it is folded along a line of symmetry. Each part represents the mirror image of the other.

#### Teaching advice
2D shape
In Stage 1, students need to have experiences involving directions and turning. Explain and discuss what represents:
a full-turn
a half-turn
a quarter-turn.
Relating this information to students physically may be helpful. For example, play games such as 'Simon Says' with Simon saying to make turns. Students may benefit from linking the vocabulary of half-turns and quarter-turns to their experiences with clocks.

Area
In covering activities, rectangular shapes are used so that students develop an understanding of the structure of the unit covering (array) in area. Knowledge of array structure is important for an understanding of area measurement. It enables the area of a rectangle to be linked to the lengths of its sides. Consequently, it provides the basis for rectangular area to be calculated using multiplication.
To assist students to develop an appreciation of the structure of repeated units in measuring area, teaching progresses from providing multiple squares to cover an area to providing only one square. Having only one square requires students to create the pattern or structure of the units by drawing or visualising.
Drawing the array structure for the tessellation of area units appears to assist students to perceive the rows (and columns) as composite units. This perception enables them to connect side length and area. If students have drawn and talked about the structure of an array, then the structure of three-dimensional stacking may be grasped more easily.

2D shape

reflection
rotation.

### Teaching advice for Three-dimensional spatial structure A
#### Content
Three-dimensional spatial structure A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Developing and retaining mental images of objects is an important skill for students. Manipulation of a variety of real three-dimensional geometric objects and two-dimensional shapes supports the development of relevant mathematical language and representation. Opportunities for these kinds of activities occur in the classroom, the playground and outside the school. Investigating the type of three-dimensional objects that are stackable makes use of the features of geometric objects in quantifying volume.
Teaching advice
Volume
The attribute of volume is the amount of space occupied by an object. Students in Stage 1 need experience in constructing volumes using informal units such as cubic blocks. Volume is formally measured in later stages using cubic units, such as:
cubic centimetres (cm3)
cubic metres (m3).
Internal volume (capacity)
Internal volume (capacity) refers to the amount a container can hold and is only used in relation to containers. It generally refers to liquid measurement, that is, the amount of liquid is equal to the internal volume of a container. Students in Stage 1 need experience in filling containers with: 
continuous materials such as water
discrete objects such as marbles.
Internal volume is formally measured in later stages using units, such as:
millilitres (mL)
litres (L).
Language
3D objects
The terms used to describe the dimensions of three-dimensional objects take different forms. Although ‘length’, ‘width’ and ‘height’ are commonly used for the three dimensions, the term ‘depth’ is sometimes used in place of height, particularly when referring to an internal dimension.
In everyday use, the context guides our choice of terms:
the ‘length’ of a piece of string
the ‘width’ of a doorway
the ‘height’ of a building
the ‘depth’ of a pool.
However, in describing an object that can readily change its orientation such as a wooden block, the choice of labels is not fixed. What would be described as the ‘height’ of the block will depend on how it is positioned.
The terms ‘length’, ‘width’ and ‘height’ can be introduced when students are engaged in using rectangular prisms.
In geometry, the term ‘face’ refers to a flat surface with only straight edges, as in prisms and pyramids. For example, a cube has six faces.
Curved surfaces, such as those found in cones, cylinders and spheres, are not classified as faces. Similarly, flat surfaces with curved boundaries, such as the circular surfaces of cones and cylinders, are not faces.
Internal volume (capacity)
When working with students in Stage 1 it is recommended that the terms ‘capacity’ and ‘internal volume’ be used interchangeably.
The internal volume of a closed container will be slightly less than its volume. Internal volume is based on the inside dimensions, while volume is determined by the outside dimensions of the container.
It is not necessary to refer to these definitions with students as capacity is not taught as a concept separate from volume until Stage 4.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
object
cube
cylinder
sphere
flat/curved surface
face
edge
prism
internal volume/capacity
has more/has less
measure.

Volume

### Teaching advice for Non-spatial measure A
#### Content
Non-spatial measure A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Measuring mass using informal units enables students to develop some key understandings of measurement. These include:
needing to repeatedly use a unit as a measuring device
selecting an appropriate unit for a specific task
appreciating that a common informal unit is necessary for comparing the masses of objects
understanding that some informal units (eg pebbles) are unsatisfactory because they are not uniform.
To have a better understanding of duration, it is important in Stage 1 that students develop a sense of:
one hour
one minute
one second.
Sense of time is developed through practical experiences, rather than simply recalling that there are 60 minutes in an hour.
Teaching advice
Mass
Mass is an intrinsic property of an object, but its most common measure is in terms of weight. Weight is a force that changes with gravity, while mass remains constant.
When students realise that changing the shape of an object does not alter its mass, they are said to conserve the property of mass.
Hefting and balancing
Hefting is testing the weight of an object by lifting and balancing it. Where possible, students can compare the weights of two objects by using their bodies to balance each object, eg holding one object in each hand. 
The equal-arm balance can have two functions:
comparing the masses of two objects
measuring the mass of an object by using multiple identical units.
Recognising that students may have different gross motor skills, a variety of experiences may need to be provided in order to support students to experience the concepts of hefting and balancing. This could include comparing the masses of two objects using grip or resistance. Students should be provided with sufficient time to process the instruction and manipulate their body appropriately in order to feel the mass of an object.
Time
In Stage 1, telling time focuses on reading the half-hour and quarter of an hour on both analog and digital clocks. While digital clocks are more common than analog clocks, the latter are more effective in representing the 60-minute base of 1 hour.
The representation of a half-hour as 30 minutes or halfway around the clockface is more transparent to the underlying base than a digital representation. The digital representation 7:30 relies on recognising that :30 means half an hour (rather than :50, for example).
The representation of a quarter past the hour as 15 minutes or one quarter around the clockface and quarter to the hour as 45 minutes or three-quarters around the clockface is a much more challenging concept than half-hour for many students. It will require extensive modelling and repeated practice.
Students need to be aware that there is always more than one way of expressing a particular time, for example:
Analog clocks can also be connected with concepts of time cycles, such as weekly, monthly, annual, seasonal or lunar cycles.
Language
As the terms ‘weigh’ and ‘weight’ are common in everyday usage, they can be accepted in student language should they arise.
An equal-arm balance consists of a pivoted horizontal lever of equal length arms, called the beam, with a weighing pan suspended from each arm.
The terms ‘hour hand’ and ‘minute hand’, rather than ‘little hand’ and ‘big hand’, should be used to promote understanding of their respective functions.

The representation of a half-hour as 30 minutes or halfway around the clockface is more transparent to the underlying base than a digital representation. The digital representation 7:30 relies on recognising that :30 means half an hour (rather than :50, for example).
The representation of a quarter past the hour as 15 minutes or one quarter around the clockface and quarter to the hour as 45 minutes or three-quarters around the clockface is a much more challenging concept than half-hour for many students. It will require extensive modelling and repeated practice.

Language
As the terms ‘weigh’ and ‘weight’ are common in everyday usage, they can be accepted in student language should they arise.
An equal-arm balance consists of a pivoted horizontal lever of equal length arms, called the beam, with a weighing pan suspended from each arm.
The terms ‘hour hand’ and ‘minute hand’, rather than ‘little hand’ and ‘big hand’, should be used to promote understanding of their respective functions.

### Teaching advice for Data A
#### Content
Data A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
In society, there is a constant need to understand, interpret and analyse information displayed in tables or graphical forms. Students need to recognise how information may be displayed in a misleading way, resulting in drawing the wrong conclusions about the data. Experiences with collecting, organising, displaying, and analysing data assist students in developing graphical and statistical literacy skills (Watson and Callingham 2003).
Early experiences with data should be based on real-life contexts relevant to the students and connected to other curriculum areas such as History, Geography, or Science and Technology. Students should be encouraged to ask questions relevant to their experiences and interests and to design ways of investigating their questions. Students develop effective data collection and display methods to present data in a variety of ways.
Teaching advice
Representing an object with a different object, picture or drawing is an abstraction.
It is important that each object in a (one-to-one) data display represents one object. One object can also represent an idea, such as a person's preference.
Aligning the rows or columns of images to form a common baseline in a picture graph can make comparisons easier.
Collecting data
When collecting information to investigate a question, students can develop simple ways of recording. Some methods include:
placing blocks or counters in a line
colouring squares on grid paper
using tally marks.
Tally marks
A single mark in a tally represents 1 observation. Tally marks are usually drawn in groups of five. The first four marks are often drawn vertical, with the fifth mark drawn diagonally through the first four. This makes counting in groups more efficient. For example:
| Tally marks | Represents |
|---|---|
|  | 3 |
|  | 5 |
|  | 9 |
Different countries use different systems of tally marks. Cultures using Chinese characters tally five by making 正.
Data in categories
Categorical data means the information being collected can be separated into distinct groups or categories. Examples include:
the colours of Smarties in a box
class members’ favourite fruits.
Students may engage more effectively with collecting data through observation or conversation, rather than questioning. Some Aboriginal students, for a number of cultural and historical reasons, may prefer not to ask or be asked direct questions, or may have well-developed observational skills that could be applied through learning activities related to collecting data.
Some students may experience difficulty accessing visual representations of data. These students can be supported by presenting data in alternate formats and through the use of assistive technology, including screen readers.
When using visual texts such as images, picture graphs, lists and tables, it is important to ensure these are accessible. Consider using:
strong colour contrast to improve visibility
alternative text and captions to provide descriptions.
Some students may also experience difficulty with conceptually understanding visual representations of data. In these instances, visual information can be introduced to students using their preferred method of communication, for example:
real objects
photos of real objects
images of objects
abstract images or drawings.
This provides opportunities for students to develop their understanding of the language used in data displays and attribute meaning between the visual representation and the data.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
information
data
collect
gather
display
symbol
tally mark
picture graph.

### Teaching advice for Chance A
#### Content
Chance A
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
A chance event is something that happens without a clear cause. Randomness is a difficult concept to grasp, even for adults. We tend to see patterns in data, even where none exist. By the end of Stage 1 students should typically be able to acknowledge the existence of a range of possible outcomes for an event. They should also be able to express the idea that some events are more likely to occur than others.
Teaching advice
Encourage students to recognise the impact of chance on predictions they make.
Certainty
When considering certainty, there are two extremes:
events that are certain to happen
events that are certain not to happen.
Words such as 'might', 'may' and 'possible' describe events between these two extremes.
Students often need assistance to develop an understanding of the terms ‘certain’ or ‘impossible’. Some students interpret events with a low probability as being impossible.
The idea of impossible, particularly in social contexts, is a more difficult concept for students than more likely or equally likely. Although we might not expect a coin toss to result in a coin standing on its edge, it is not impossible. It is the aggregation of all of the possible outcomes that really means an event is certain or impossible.
Sample space
To address notions of chance, it is often best to use examples where all the things that can happen, sometimes called the sample space, are well defined. Students often do not realise that for something to be impossible there must be none present at all in the sample space, the set of all possible outcomes. Determining the sample space (that is, what is possible) is the essential first step in understanding any probability problem.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
possible
might happen
likely/unlikely/more likely.
