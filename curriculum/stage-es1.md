## Teaching advice for Early Stage 1
### Teaching advice for Representing whole numbers
#### Content
Representing whole numbers
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Representing numbers in a variety of ways is essential for developing number sense.
Students can represent numbers using:
| Representation type | Example |
|---|---|
| Number words | five |
| Numerals | 5 |
| Finger patterns |  |
| Collections of perceptual items | 5 pencils |
As a student learns to represent whole numbers, they begin to move between the number words used in counting, numerals and quantity. Students could use concrete materials, gestures (such as nodding) or body tallying (Butterworth 1999) to indicate the count. A student should be able to select a given number of items (more than five) from a larger collection if the number is represented by a word or symbol. For example, a student is shown the numeral 8, and counts out eight items from a collection of twenty items.
The cardinality principle recognises that the last number name represents the total number in the collection. This principle marks a profound conceptual achievement, understanding how counting works (Paliwal and Baroody 2018).
Teaching advice
Counting
Counting is an important component of number knowledge and the early learning of operations. In Early Stage 1, typical development would be for students to be able to count to 30 and elaborate the count by stating the next number without restarting the count.
Counting develops over time as number words are assigned progressively more sophisticated meanings. The process of learning to count, which starts with memorising a few words of the counting list and continues to the point of understanding the cardinality principle, is the process by which students develop their first mental representations of exact, larger numerical quantities (as high as they can count) (Sarnecka et al. 2015).
Counting with understanding
Counting with understanding involves:
counting with one-to-one correspondence (one-to-one principle)
recognising that the last number name represents the total number in the collection (cardinal principle).
Other counting principles include:
stable-order principle: you always say number words in a particular sequence when counting
order-irrelevance principle: the order in which you count items does not change the total counted
abstraction principle: it does not matter what you count, numbers can be used to count anything and the process for counting is always the same.
There is a distinction between counting by rote and counting with understanding. A student might produce the correct sequence of number words to 15. The same student may not understand the one-to-one correspondence between the number words and the number of objects. It is important to assess one-to-one correspondence and cardinality within a student’s counting range.
When students are learning to count, they can often produce a higher rote count than the largest collection they can count to with understanding. This is why the rote count expectation is at least 30, while students are expected to only represent numbers to at least 20.
Explicit modelling and multiple opportunities for guided and independent practice can support the development of the cardinality principle. Counting a set of objects followed by emphasising the last word counted, and then repeating the final word in the count while gesturing to the corresponding objects can support this understanding (Paliwal and Baroody 2018).
Distinguishing between teens and multiples of ten
The teen numbers in English are often the most difficult for students. The oral language of teen numbers is the reverse of the usual pattern of 'tens first and then ones'.
Students should learn to distinguish between commonly confused similar-sounding numerals, such as 13 and 30 or 15 and 50. Phonemic awareness can assist students in moving between numerals and number words (Lopes-Silva et al. 2014).
Although students in Early Stage 1 are not expected to count sequentially beyond 30, many students may be able to count by tens (10, 20, 30, 40, 50 …). It is important to make the distinction between the suffixes teen and ty as early as possible to reduce the risk of confusing number names when counting higher.
Subitising
The word 'subitise' is derived from Latin and means 'to arrive suddenly'. Subitising is immediately recognising the number of objects in a small (up to four) random collection without having to count the objects. For up to four items, response times increase by about 40–100 ms for every dot, while for larger numbers they increase by 250–350 ms per dot, leading to a clear change in the slope of the response curve (Mandler and Shebo 1982).
It enables us to use grouping structures when establishing the number of items in larger collections.
Perceptual subitising has two different forms:
preverbal subitising – does not require naming the number of objects. For example, infants are thought to use preverbal subitising to distinguish between magnitudes, before they learn to speak (Tan and Bryant 2000).
verbal subitising – number words express quantity.
Pattern recognition, sometimes referred to as ‘conceptual subitising’ (Clements, 1999), can assist students to develop capabilities of recognising units and combining numbers.
We can also recognise larger numbers of dots when presented in a known pattern (such as the figure below). Many students may initially see this as groups of three.

To support the development of subitising, students should develop an understanding that quantities have names. Students should understand that number words have useful purposes for counting and for comparisons (Staves 2019). 
Students who have already demonstrated the cardinality principle and subitising could be extended in their learning by focusing on the pattern structure of numbers through dice and domino patterns and through representing quantities to 20.
Language
The oral count starts as a fixed recitation of number words. It then progresses through overlapping phases of acquiring and elaborating the counting sequence. In the elaboration phase, students learn to continue the count from any point, identifying the number word that comes before or after any counting word.
The challenges of learning the teen number words in English mean that students can often identify which number word comes before twenty-three, in advance of identifying the number word before thirteen.
Languages other than English often have different patterns for number words. Acknowledging the structure of number words in students’ home language will be important in supporting students to understand representations of number in English. Explicit teaching of patterns of number words in English, paired with verbal and visual representations, can assist.

Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to investigate if a student can read and represent whole numbers to at least 20.
Provide the student with a shuffled set of number cards (1 to 20).
Ask the student to put the numbers in order from smallest to largest. For some students, ordering the cards may include gesture, eye gaze, or verbally telling the teacher.
If the student is unsuccessful, ask the student to count for you. Have the student start from one or two numbers before the initial error in ordering the cards.
If the student is successful, indicate different cards and ask, What number is that?
How the student responds to these questions will provide information on which numerals (1 to 20) they can identify. You could assess numeral recognition by asking the student to indicate various numbers as you say them.
For students with diverse needs this task could be adapted for determining numeral recognition, identification and meaning. This could be done using matching activities, where students are presented with a sample (for example the numeral 5) and a number of pictures to select from (including one correct image). Start with one or two incorrect pictures, increasing as the student becomes proficient. Incorrect pictures could include blank cards, images of larger or smaller quantities and then numbers.
To check if a student demonstrates an understanding of how whole numbers indicate quantity, cover over the first five cards. Ask the student:
How many numbers have I covered up?
[Prompt, if needed] Look at the numbers that are left.
How do you know?
Does the student need to count from one to determine how many are covered? Or does the student recognise that if 6 is visible, 1 to 5 are covered?
Reasoning that five cards have been covered, based on what remains uncovered, suggests an understanding of how whole numbers indicate quantity.

### Teaching advice for Combining and separating quantities
#### Content
Combining and separating quantities
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Addition and subtraction develop from students counting, combining and separating objects. In time, they learn to use number words or numerals as replacements for completed counts.
The processes of addition and subtraction are developed together to provide the foundation for the understanding of their inverse relationship. Reasoning about the way numbers can be combined and separated supports understanding the actions of adding and subtracting as inverse operations.
Teaching advice
While addition covers combining two or more groups, subtraction covers two different situations:
taking away from a group
comparing two groups.
Students should first be able to compare groups of objects by matching, using one-to-one correspondence. They then determine how many more or how many fewer objects there are in a group.
Students need many experiences that require them to relate their knowledge to the vocabulary and conceptual frameworks of mathematics. Visualising, modelling, drawing and writing mathematical problems can provide these experiences when combining and separating quantities.
Storytelling is an effective way for students to conceptualise and communicate mathematical ideas, such as combining and separating quantities, and assists students to recognise the purposeful nature of mathematics in real-world contexts. Mathematics as storytelling (Matthews 2008) can support the engagement and achievement of Aboriginal students, and all students.
Formal writing of number sentences, including the use of the symbols +, − and =, is introduced in Stage 1.
Reasoning about number relations
Reasoning about number relations involves students being able to discuss how addition and subtraction are related and how they are different. Reasoning in this case involves appreciating why addition and subtraction are inverse or ‘opposite’ operations.
Reasoning about inverse relationships
Students reason about number relations when modelling addition and subtraction through gestures and/or words as they combine and separate groups of items. Students notice that if they have ten items, they can separate the items into two piles, say four and six, then combine them again, and still have ten items. Students might also notice that they can remove four items from the pile of ten and have six (subtraction), then move the six back to have ten (addition). These actions, combining and separating, are ‘opposite’ to each other.
Reasoning about number relationships
Students may know that 5 + 4 and 4 + 5 are ‘turn around facts’ but may not be able to give reasons why they result in the same total. Modelling what these number relations look like assists in building the generalisation that it does not matter which order you add the numbers, the total is still the same (the commutative property of addition).
Building reasoning also needs to include opportunities to reflect on how this process is not true for subtraction. Understanding that we cannot change the order of numbers for subtraction is an important concept. Investigating what happens when you have three items and try to take away five items, and there not being enough items to complete the task, is an important step in reasoning about number relations.
Relations and relationships
The syllabus refers to students establishing number relations and also working with relationships between numbers. The terms ‘relation’ and ‘relationship’ have very similar meanings when used as nouns. ‘Relation’ means the manner in which two things may be associated. ‘Relationship’ means connection or association. For example, there is a relation between the numbers 3, 5 and 8, and their relationship is additive.
Some Aboriginal students may have well-developed conceptual understandings of quantitative ‘relations’ and ‘relationships’ based on cultural knowledge and practices, such as connections within Country, kinship systems or community structures.
Language
Some students may need assistance when two tenses are used in the one problem. For example, I had six beans and took away four. How many do I have now?
Students may be supported by the provision of a real-world context or story to clarify the use of past and present tense. For example, I had six beans and my brother took away four to give to our cousins. How many do I have now?
Questions involving comparison subtraction (how many more?) are much more difficult than determining which is more? Students may just state the higher number when asked how many more. In part, students find comparison subtraction difficult because they are not familiar with the question type. Comparison subtraction requires students to determine the difference between two quantities.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate: 
count forwards
combines with
joins
count backwards
take away
how many more
altogether
makes.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to investigate if a student can reason about number relations by combining and separating or comparing collections of objects. Note that the number of counters may need to be adjusted for students with a lower counting range.
Present the student with a pile of 15 counters or similar objects. For some students visual images or assistive technology may be required.
Ask the student:
Can you get me 8 counters from the pile? How do you know that is 8?
Are there more counters here [point at the 8 counters] or more counters there [point at the 7 remaining]? How do you know?
If you put them all together, how many are there?
Typical development by the end of Early Stage 1 would be for students to be able to count (with one-to-one correspondence) to determine a total within the range of 1 to 20.

### Teaching advice for Forming groups
#### Content
Forming groups
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
The process of sharing objects equally and putting shared objects back together to form one collection develops students’ understanding of the link between multiplication and division and provides the foundation for a conceptual understanding of their inverse relationship. In Early Stage 1, students develop strategies for forming groups based on the structure and pattern of numbers as countable units. Strategies related to forming groups in the early years are foundational to the multiplicative structure of number (Mendes and Ferreira 2008).
Reasoning about patterns
In Early Stage 1, reasoning about relations between pattern units involves recognising the pattern unit and being able to continue the pattern (Samara and Clements 2009). Starting with two-part patterns AB, students should be encouraged to extend, correct and create patterns. Once students can recognise a two-part pattern, they should learn to fill in missing elements in a pattern, and then copy and extend a pattern. In forming groups in Early Stage 1, students learn to connect the processes of patterning within number. This is foundational knowledge for learning algebra in later stages.
Teaching advice
Forming groups should, wherever possible, engage students in manipulating concrete materials. Alternatively, visual or digital forms of modelling groups may be used by students with complex learning needs. The emphasis is on modelling groups of the same size and describing them.
Students initially distribute objects one by one until the supply is exhausted. Teachers should draw attention to the number of groups and the number in each group. After students have shared objects equally, the process can be reversed, putting the shared objects back together to form one collection.
There are two processes associated with forming groups. The first looks at sharing when students know the number of groups. The second involves finding the number of groups when you know how many are in each group.
| Process | Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Equal sharing | Partitive | Distributing items one at a time into a set number of groups. | A student has a number of pop sticks and three cups. They share out the pop sticks into the cups one at a time. |
| Equal grouping | Quotitive | Distributing the same number of items into an unknown number of groups. | A student has 12 pop sticks and wants to make groups of four. They place four pop sticks down, then another four, and so on. |
Forming groups of objects that have the same number of elements helps students develop the mathematical concept of equality.
Students who have already demonstrated forming equal groups by sharing and counting collections of objects could be extended in their learning by:
posing problems or questions that can be solved by forming groups
using their understanding of forming equal groups to represent a number in different ways, for example 12 as 3 groups of 4, 4 groups of 3, 6 groups of 2, or 2 groups of 6.
Using familiar objects to represent equal groups and patterns can be useful in supporting student understanding of these concepts. It is important to keep in mind that students come from diverse backgrounds and experiences, and objects that are considered to be familiar to one student may not be familiar to another.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate: 
group
share
equal
pattern
repeat.
Check that students are familiar with groups of two like things being called pairs.
Suggestions for determining key progress points
The following activity can be used to investigate if a student can form equal groups by sharing and counting collections of objects.
Provide the student with a pile of at least 20 linking cubes or similar objects.
Ask:
Can you make 5 towers, with 3 blocks in each tower?
How many towers do you have?
How many in each tower?
How many altogether?

### Teaching advice for Geometric measure
#### Content
Geometric measure
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Learning about position is important in locating one’s self and others in space. The words used in describing position are the basis of the prepositions in English, such as ‘in’, ‘on’ or ‘under’. In the early years, learning about length is key to developing an understanding of proximity and provides a useful context for learning about fractions.
Teaching advice
Position
The language and associated vocabulary developed regarding position are important both within the topic of position itself, and for use with other mathematical concepts. Words and phrases such as ‘next to’, ‘before’ and ‘after’ can be used both in a physical world context and also in more abstract contexts. For example, in position – the chair is next to the table or the teacher entered the room before the students, whereas in number – 13 comes before 14, or 20 is next to 19 on the chart.
Positional language should be addressed regularly to enable students to develop a deep understanding of when and how to use these words. A variety of physical, digital and visual representations are helpful for some phrases such as ‘on top of’. On top of can be difficult as students may be confused between whether it means superimposed or above (see images below).
There are two main ideas associated with position for students in Early Stage 1:
following an instruction to position an object or themselves
describing the relative position of an object or themselves.
Some students may be able to describe the position of an object in relation to themselves, but not in relation to another object. The concept of position has strong vocabulary demands that are detailed below.
Ordinals
Ordinal number words such as ‘first’, ‘second’ and ‘third' represent a position in a sequence. They are different from whole numbers, which represent quantity. Ordinal numbers are introduced in relevant contexts, such as games, storytelling or recording the date.
Left and right
In Early Stage 1, students learn to use the terms ‘left’ and ‘right’ to describe position in relation to themselves. Teachers can assist students’ understanding of left and right by referencing a dominant hand or foot. The terms ‘left’ and ‘right’ depend on the frame of reference (which way an individual is facing) and can be a source of confusion for several years.
Typically, students are not expected to use ‘left’ or ‘right’ to describe the position of an object from the perspective of a person facing in the opposite direction until Stage 1. However, students who have mastered the concept of left and right in relation to themselves might be introduced to this idea.
Some students with perceptual difficulties and poor spatial awareness may experience difficulties with the concept of position. Modelling, and the use of visual cues, can support these students to follow or describe position.
Conversely, some students have well-developed skills in applying the concept of position, even if they do not yet have the language to articulate it. For example, some Aboriginal students may demonstrate a high degree of spatial awareness on a larger scale, such as in the local area or on-Country. Students may be supported by explicit teaching of mathematical language associated with spatial positioning.
Length
Early Stage 1 students often develop an awareness of the attribute of length by comparing objects of different lengths. Early Stage 1 focuses on the importance of accurately aligning one end of each object being compared. Where possible, students need experience with physically aligning the ends of two objects to compare their lengths. Start with comparing objects where the difference in lengths is easy to notice, for example the table is longer than the pencil. 
When items are closer in length, aligning the ends becomes necessary. For example comparing the height of two students by standing them back-to-back or aligning the ends of the two pencils.
Conservation of length is an important concept. It develops over time when students are comparing lengths. If students consistently say that the objects are equal in length, though their relative positions have been altered, they are conserving length.
Introducing fractions through length
Many students find understanding fractions, and particularly fraction magnitudes, to be one of the most challenging concepts in mathematics. The concept of a fraction is based on the process of division, rather than counting parts. To assist students in developing the process of division and its relationship to fractions, the continuous linear model of fractions is addressed in the concept of length.
The syllabus introduces fractions through equal sharing, using continuous and discrete models. This lays the foundations for fractions as indicating division and fractions as numbers.
Half
Half can be interpreted as dividing one whole into two equal pieces (continuous) or dividing a collection into two equal groups (discrete). The process is applied in early models of halving lengths and discrete quantities.
See Forming groups for discrete models of fractions.
The idea of a halfway point builds on the equality of two lengths. It also introduces the notion of subdividing a length, which in turn underpins measuring. It is important to model and reinforce the language of two equal lengths when describing ‘halfway’ and to provide non-examples where the lengths are not equal.
Further advice is available in Introducing fractions: Sharing (linked with division).
Some students with poor visual perception and difficulties with fine motor skills may require additional support to manipulate items and compare lengths. Strategies that can support these students include:
using visual cues such as a sticker or object to indicate the start and end of lengths
outlining boundaries of an image in bold
selecting colours that give a good colour contrast between background and foreground if using images of lengths
participating in activities that require hand–eye coordination.
Students who have already demonstrated an understanding of length and half a length, could be extended in their learning by finding other fractions of lengths such as repeatedly halving to find quarters, or exploring folding to make thirds.
Language
Students may need practice with the language of length in a variety of contexts. Young students often use general terms such as ‘big’ when they mean ‘tall’. They may know the word ‘fat’, but not the word ‘thick’. The terms ‘big’ and ‘long’ are sometimes used for specific attributes such as:
height
width
depth
length
thickness.
Engage students in activities that develop clarity in expressing distinct mathematical concepts. Providing examples and non-examples, including through the use of the Frayer model, can support students’ understanding of these mathematical concepts. See Vocabulary and language in Mathematics K–6 (Word, 16 pages, 1.14MB) for a further explanation, examples and non-examples.
Teachers can support students to use the language of mathematics by developing understanding that the same word can be used in a range of contexts. Some students may use ‘big’ to signify importance or ‘biggest’ to indicate intensity, such as those who use Aboriginal English. Making connections between concepts, and ensuring that students know when the context is mathematical, can support students to select the most appropriate term to use.
Vocabulary

inside/outside

directions

### Teaching advice for Two-dimensional spatial structure
#### Content
Two-dimensional spatial structure
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
The development of two-dimensional spatial structure includes an ability to identify, rotate, orient and visualise shapes. These spatial skills are foundational for developing mathematical understanding related to the array structure of multiplication and area, map interpretation, visualisation and geometric reasoning (Lowrie et al. 2021). 
Features and properties of shapes also contribute to understanding the connection between two-dimensional shapes and three-dimensional objects and spaces.
Students entering Early Stage 1 typically bring an understanding of shapes, which includes both regular and irregular shapes. In Early Stage 1, students also develop an awareness of the attribute of area through covering activities. Students’ knowledge and skills in spatial reasoning develop as they engage with content related to shape and area.
Teaching advice
2D shape
Students should be given time to explore and represent shapes in a range of contexts, such as:
identifying shapes in the classroom, school and local environment
representing shapes through painting, drawing, movements, gestures, writing, cutting and pasting.
It is important to select or create images of shapes that give a good colour contrast between background and foreground. This will assist students to see the images clearly and support students with visual difficulties.
Features of shapes
Children first learn that the question, What shape is this? is answered by words such as ‘circle’ and ‘square’. They then associate these shape words with initial examples. The initial examples contribute to the prototype image students hold for a shape. The equilateral triangle is a common prototype image for a triangle. This has meant that some students identify a right-angled triangle as half a triangle (Hourigan and Leavy 2015). For this reason it is important for teachers to introduce all types of triangles.
To develop a robust concept of shapes it is important that students encounter shapes in different orientations and sizes, represented in a variety of ways. Templates from which students create shapes can help to reinforce their understanding of the features of shapes and support developing students’ skills in reasoning about spatial relations.
Non-examples
In addition to showing students examples of a shape, providing non-examples of the shape helps focus their attention on the critical attributes. The non-examples should be similar to the examples, but without the defining characteristic of the shapes.
For example, a triangle with the defining characteristic of the number of sides, could be compared to:
| Shape Name | Shape |
|---|---|
| Chevron |  |
| Kite |  |
Students who require additional support to identify shapes may first need to be introduced to non-examples with significantly different characteristics. Match-to-sample activities can also support the understanding of the features of particular shapes. Students can be presented with a correct sample and one or two non-examples to begin with, with the number of non-examples increasing as students’ understanding develops.
In Early Stage 1, students should work with asymmetric or irregular shapes as well as regular shapes. Students are not expected to use the terms ‘regular’ and ‘irregular’ themselves.
See Vocabulary and language in Mathematics K–6 (Word, 16 pages, 1.14MB) for a further explanation of teaching concepts using examples and non-examples.
Area
Students should be given opportunities to compare:
two similar shapes of different areas where one fits inside the boundary of the other
two different-shaped areas where one can be placed on top of the other
two different shapes with the same area where one shape can be cut up and pasted onto the other.
Students who have already demonstrated an understanding of area could be extended in their learning by:
comparing shapes with similar areas that cannot be superimposed on each other
predicting and finding two shapes that might have the same area
learning to measure area.
Language
The term ‘shape’ is used in the syllabus to refer to a two-dimensional figure. ‘Object’ is used to refer to a three-dimensional solid or geometric form.
There are two different strategies that students can use to compare area by placing one on top of another. These are: superimposing and superposing.
Superimpose: comparison of areas by placing one area on top of another. 
Superpose: comparison of areas by aligning the edges (or corners) of two areas when one is placed on top of the other.

### Teaching advice for Three-dimensional spatial structure
#### Content
Three-dimensional spatial structure
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Concepts related to three-dimensional space are important to functioning in the world. Three-dimensional spatial structure is foundational for developing skills and processes related to position and location, perspective, and directions.
The manipulation of a variety of 3D objects supports the development of visual imagery, language and representation. Manipulation of 3D objects and observing them from different viewpoints leads to the development of skills in identifying the structural features of objects.
Students learn to understand volume as related to the measurement of three-dimensional space, in the same way that area relates to the measurement of two-dimensional space. Students need to explore the concept of volume as part of identifying and quantifying attributes of an object. Learning to compare internal volumes of containers and volumes of objects (how much space they take up) develops important estimation skills.
Teaching advice
3D geometric objects
In Early Stage 1, the emphasis is on students manipulating, describing, sorting and representing familiar geometric objects. Teachers are encouraged to use a range of objects of different sizes. Some students may require larger objects to manipulate.
Students should also be encouraged to describe the features of familiar objects using the terms ‘flat’, ‘round’ or ‘curved’. This prepares students to sort objects based on particular attributes, such as size, shape or function.
In developing students’ skills in reasoning about spatial relations, students should engage in tasks where they predict the stacking capabilities of various objects.
Volume and internal volume (capacity)
The attribute of volume is the amount of space occupied by an object or substance. The internal volume or capacity is only used in relation to containers and generally refers to liquid measurement. Knowing these definitions is not an expectation at this stage in learning.
In Early Stage 1, comparisons are made directly, using methods such as pouring or packing the contents of one container into another. Students can engage in pouring and filling containers with water or sand.
Early experiences often lead students to the conclusion that taller containers hold more. To develop beyond this, students need to directly compare containers that are:
shorter and hold more
taller and hold less
shorter and hold less
taller and hold more
short and hold the same as a tall container.
Many opportunities to emphasise the concepts of volume and capacity occur throughout the school day.
Students may be involved in stacking objects:
packing up toys
making models
storing objects in cupboards.
Students who have already demonstrated an understanding of volume could be extended in their learning by exploring different ways of stacking 24 cubes (eg 3 by 2 by 4 or 6 by 2 by 2).
Language
Students often need meaningful practice in using the general word container to include objects such as bottles, jars and tubs.

### Teaching advice for Non-spatial measure
#### Content
Non-spatial measure
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
The units of measurement in Mass (g, kg) and Time (min, sec) do not rely on a physical spatial structure. In Mass, the spatial arrangement of the units does not affect the total weight. This is different to area or length where the arrangement of the units matter – they need to be end to end with no gaps.
In Early Stage 1, it is important that students develop an awareness of the attribute of mass and some of the language used to describe mass.
Concepts of time and duration are influenced by students’ experiences, cultural knowledge and personal judgements. Time can be understood and represented in a range of ways such as lunar, seasonal and Gregorian calendar systems, or linear and cyclical concepts of time.
Teaching advice
While Mass and Time are organised under the same substrand, it is not intended that teachers make connections across concepts in each of Time and Mass.
Mass
Early experiences often lead students to the conclusion that:
large things are heavier than small things, and
if two things are the same size and shape, they will have the same mass.
However, mass is not a spatial measure. Objects with the same volume can have different masses.
Students in Early Stage 1 should only be asked to compare two objects that are quite different in mass. Students need experiences with objects that are:
light and large
heavy and large
light and small
heavy and small
large but lighter than a smaller object.
Recognising that students may have different gross motor skills, teachers may need to provide a variety of experiences in order to support students to experience mass. These experiences could include:
feeling resistance against the body to determine if something is heavy or light
using grip to identify the mass of an object, using the terms ‘lighter’ or ‘heavier’
placing an object, such as a blanket, on the student’s lap to determine if the object is light or heavy.
Students who have already demonstrated an understanding of mass could be extended in their learning by exploring the placement of mass at different points on an equal-arm balance.
Time
Duration
An understanding of duration is introduced through ideas such as:
before
after
how long
how soon.
In Early Stage 1 time spans are personal judgements. Moments in time include ideas such as:
daytime
today
days of the week.
Students can be supported to develop an understanding of time by introducing time in everyday contexts, for example:
wait times for activities
duration of preferred activities, eg using timers
schedule of familiar routines, eg first-and-then board, visual schedule
identifying days of the week through regular weekly routines, eg sports, music
identifying activities done before and after school, eg night-time routines.
Telling time
In Early Stage 1, telling time focuses on reading hour time on analog and digital clocks. It draws on:
numeral recognition
position
spatial comparison.
Analog and digital clocks can be shown either through real objects or visual representations. When engaging with analog or digital clocks, it is important to ensure these displays are accessible. Consider using:
strong colour contrast between the hour and minute hands to improve visibility
braille, tactile or talking clocks.
Students who have already demonstrated an understanding of time could be extended in their learning by representing times on analog and digital clocks.
Schools are encouraged to work in partnership with their local Aboriginal community(ies) to develop understanding about Aboriginal concepts of time and associated language and terminology. This can assist teachers to support students in developing their understanding of content related to time.
Language
The terms ‘weigh’ and ‘weight’ are more common in everyday usage than ‘mass’. When working with students in Early Stage 1 ‘weight’ is an acceptable description for mass. Weight is a force that changes with gravity, while mass remains constant.
Hefting is testing the weight of an object by lifting or holding and balancing it. Where possible, students can use their bodies to balance two objects and compare the weight of each, for example holding one object in each hand.
The words ‘long’ and ‘short’ can be confusing to students who have only experienced these words in terms of length measurement. Students need experience with these words in both the context of length and time. Teachers should be mindful that the terms ‘long’ and ‘short’ are comparatives. Students might consider 5 years, 5 000 years, or 50 000 years to be a long time.
Some students, including Aboriginal students, may distinguish historical from contemporary time scales. For example, students may use additional qualifiers or alternative terms, such as ‘very long’, ‘long, long time ago’ or ‘old time’.

### Teaching advice for Data
#### Content
Data
#### Teaching advice
Why is it important?
Describing the data and its spread, as well as creating data displays, draws upon students’ skills in quantitative and spatial reasoning. Comparing probabilities relies on and can support the development of proportional reasoning. When students need to reason beyond the available data, the ideas of chance or likelihood are introduced. Further, comparing probabilities relies on proportional reasoning.
Teaching advice
In Early Stage 1, students collect information about themselves and their environment with teacher assistance. They use objects as data and group these objects into a data display. Objects should be grouped according to characteristics, such as colour, size, shape or texture. Students begin to reason about relations by applying their counting knowledge and skills in comparing the size of different groups of objects.
As students learn to predict responses to and respond to questions about information collected, they begin to develop an introductory understanding of probability. Probability can be a rather challenging concept to grasp as the outcome of many events cannot be predicted with total certainty. Developing a widely acceptable definition of probability took nearly three centuries and was marked by much controversy. In simple terms, probability is how likely something is to happen.
Probability can appear to work against our sense of balance. If a coin has landed heads five times in a row, we expect it to be more likely to turn up tails on the next throw. The idea that what has happened in the past has no impact on the future is challenging for students to understand at this stage of learning. The understanding of chance situations is developed through the use of simple experiments which produce data so that students can make comparisons. However, students’ personal beliefs, such as favourite colours, influence their predictions of chance events. In Early Stage 1, some chance experiments may be included as part of an investigation that collects data.
Students will need support in understanding the key vocabulary demands associated with probability, provided below.
Language
The language of chance is introduced in Early Stage 1 through discussions of data collected. Discussing possible outcomes of data collections can help clarify events that are certain and those that are not.
Question starters include:
Could it be …?
Why couldn’t it be …?
Using events that are familiar to students will support their understanding of events that are certain and events that are not certain.
Vocabulary
Students should be provided with opportunities to develop confidence in using some or all of the following terms to communicate:
information
collect
group
display
objects.
